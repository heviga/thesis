\chapter{Practical Part}
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:practical_introduction}

This chapter focuses on the practical implementation of modeling and control strategies for a plate heat exchanger using the Armfield Process Plant Trainer PCT23 \cite{armfield_pct23_manual}. It presents the procedures for system identification, controller design, and the evaluation of control performance through simulations and real-time experiments.

The Armfield PCT23 is a laboratory-scale plate heat exchanger consisting of two hydraulically separated circuits: a hot fluid circuit and a cold fluid circuit. Heat transfer between the two media takes place in the plate heat exchanger, while the overall process dynamics are influenced by the flow rates and inlet temperatures of both circuits. Owing to the characteristics of heat transfer, the system exhibits slow dynamics and nonlinear behavior typical of thermal processes.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{images/PU.png}
    \caption{Armfield Process Plant Trainer PCT23. (1) Plate heat exchanger, 
    (2) cold water reservoir, 
    (3) hot water reservoir with electric heating, 
    (4) Pump~W for the cold water circuit, 
    (5) Pump~F for the hot water circuit.}
    \label{fig:pct23_plant}
\end{figure}

The hot medium is heated by an electric heater, whose power is regulated by a dedicated controller to maintain the inlet temperature of the hot circuit at an approximately constant level. In this work, the heater control is treated as an auxiliary control loop and is not included in the main control design.

A photograph of the experimental setup is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:pct23_plant}. The plate heat exchanger is indicated by label~(1). The hot water circuit is driven by the heating pump, shown as component~(5), which supplies heated water to the heat exchanger. The remaining components include the hot and cold water reservoirs and the corresponding circulation pumps.

The controlled variable is the outlet temperature from the heat exchanger, denoted as $T_4$. The manipulated variable is the pump power of the hot circuit (Pump~F), which directly affects the flow rate and, consequently, the heat transfer rate. The control input is constrained by the physical limits of the pump within the range of $20$--$100~\%$.

The objective of the practical part is to identify suitable models of the process based on experimental data, design MPC controllers using both a classical first-order Strejc model and a data-driven Koopman model, and compare their performance under identical constraints in simulation and real-time operation on the PCT23 system.




\section{System Identification}

System identification experiments were conducted to obtain input--output data suitable for the development of both classical and data-driven models of the heat exchanger. The experiments were performed on the Armfield PCT23 setup described in the previous section.

Before the identification measurements, the heat exchanger was operated until steady conditions were achieved. The temperature of the hot circuit was regulated by the built-in electric heater, whose power was controlled by a simple proportional controller. The controller gain was selected such that the inlet temperature of the hot circuit was maintained close to a steady-state value. Throughout all identification experiments, the heater control settings were kept unchanged to ensure consistent thermal conditions. This auxiliary control loop was not modified and is therefore not considered part of the identification process.

During the identification experiments, the pump supplying the hot circuit (Pump~F) was used as the manipulated input. The pump speed was changed in steps every $250~\mathrm{s}$ to capture the dynamic response of the system.

The pump of the cold circuit (Pump~W) was operated at a constant speed of $50~\%$ throughout the experiments. This ensured a steady flow rate of the cold medium and allowed the influence of the hot-side flow rate on the outlet temperature to be isolated.

The measured output used for identification was the temperature at the outlet of the heat exchanger, denoted as $T_4$. The pump speed and the corresponding outlet temperature were recorded continuously. Figure~\ref{fig:raw_measurements} shows the applied input signal (Pump~F speed) and the resulting outlet temperature response used for system identification.

\subsection{Step-Response-Based Identification of the Heat Exchanger}
\label{sec:strejc_ident}

An average normalized step response was obtained by averaging all valid normalized responses from the identification dataset. This averaged response provides a representative description of the dominant process dynamics and is used for the identification of a simple linear model. The resulting average step response is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:avg_step}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/fig_average_step_response.png}
    \caption{Average normalized step response used for linear model identification.}
    \label{fig:avg_step}
\end{figure}

Based on the averaged step response, a first-order linear model was identified. The steady-state gain $K$ was estimated from the final value of the response, while the time constant $\tau$ was determined using the $63.2\%$ criterion.

For the identified process, the estimated parameters were
\[
K \approx 1.17, \qquad \tau \approx 68~\text{samples}.
\]

With a sampling period of $T_s = 1~\mathrm{s}$, the continuous-time model was converted into a discrete-time state-space representation. For a first-order system, the discrete-time coefficients are given by
\begin{equation}
A = e^{-T_s/\tau}, \qquad
B = K \left(1 - e^{-T_s/\tau}\right), \qquad
C = 1, \qquad
D = 0.
\end{equation}

Substituting the identified parameters into the discrete-time model introduced in equation~\eqref{eq:strejc_state} yields the following representation:
\begin{equation}
x_{k+1} = 0.985\,x_k + 0.016\,u_k,
\qquad
y_k = x_k.
\end{equation}

The identified linear model is subsequently used as the prediction model in the baseline MPC design.

To evaluate the predictive capability of the model, an open-loop simulation was performed using the testing dataset. In this setting, the measured input signal was applied directly to the model, while the output was not corrected using measurements. The resulting trajectory therefore represents a pure model prediction driven only by the input signal and the initial condition.

The model parameters were identified using the complete dataset. For validation, the data were split, and the second part of the experiment, starting at $t = 2250~\mathrm{s}$, was used for open-loop testing.

The predicted output $\hat{y}_k$ was compared with the measured outlet temperature $y_k = T_4$, and the prediction error was quantified using the root mean square error (RMSE),
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{RMSE} =
\sqrt{\frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=1}^{N} \left( y_k - \hat{y}_k \right)^2},
\end{equation}
where $N$ denotes the number of samples in the testing dataset. The RMSE was computed in physical units after reversing the data scaling.

For the identified linear model, the overall open-loop RMSE obtained on the test data was
\[
\mathrm{RMSE}_{\mathrm{OL}} = 1.6962\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}.
\]

This value provides a direct measure of model accuracy, as it reflects how well the model predicts the process output without feedback. The results indicate that the model captures the dominant thermal dynamics of the heat exchanger, while prediction errors gradually accumulate due to nonlinear effects and unmodeled disturbances.

Although the model is sufficient for basic control design, these limitations motivate the use of more expressive data-driven models, such as the Koopman-based approach introduced in the following sections.

\subsection{Koopman-based Identification of a Heat Exchanger}
\label{sec:koopman_identification}

This subsection summarizes how the Koopman prediction model used later for control was obtained. While Section~\ref{sec:Koopman_operator} presents the general Koopman framework, here we focus on the specific model structure, training setup, and how the final state-space matrices were extracted.

The identification was implemented in the \texttt{Neuromancer} framework~\cite{neuromancer2022}. Before training, the measured output and input signals were standardized using precomputed \texttt{StandardScaler} objects (mean and standard deviation), mainly to improve numerical conditioning. The scaling parameters used in this work were
\[
\mu_y = 59.0676, \qquad \sigma_y = 6.9122,
\]
\[
\mu_u = 65.8447, \qquad \sigma_u = 22.9062.
\]



We used an encoder--decoder architecture with control inputs. The measured outlet temperature $y_k$ was lifted to a latent state $z_k \in \mathbb{R}^{n_z}$ with $n_z=10$ using a fully connected multi-layer perceptron with three hidden layers $[60,\,60,\,60]$ and ReLU activations. The input $u_k$ was mapped to the same latent dimension by a linear layer without bias. The latent dynamics were represented by a linear operator of the form
\[
z_{k+1} = K z_k + B_{\mathcal K} u_k,
\]
where $K$ corresponds to a trainable linear layer and $B_{\mathcal K}$ is implicitly given by the input lifting layer. The output was reconstructed by a linear decoder without bias,
\[
\hat{y}_k = C_{\mathcal K} z_k.
\]
The Koopman matrices used later in MPC correspond directly to the learned network weights. The total number of trainable parameters was $8170$.

Training was performed on segments of length $n_{\mathrm{steps}}=50$ using mini-batches of size $b_s=100$. We used the Adam optimizer with learning rate $10^{-3}$ and a fixed random seed. The maximum number of epochs was set to $900$, with early stopping monitored on the development loss (patience $200$) and a warmup period of $100$ epochs. Training took approximately $35$ minutes on a single GPU.

Training minimized a weighted sum of loss terms that penalize prediction errors and enforce consistency of the lifted representation. In contrast to the general formulation presented in Section~\ref{sec:Koopman_operator}, the practical implementation used the following weights:
\begin{itemize}
    \item multi-step output prediction loss weighted by $10$,
    \item one-step prediction loss weighted by $1$,
    \item reconstruction loss weighted by $20$,
    \item latent consistency loss weighted by $1$.
\end{itemize}

These loss terms correspond to the objectives of accurate multi-step prediction, local one-step accuracy, encoder--decoder consistency, and linear evolution in the lifted space. The corresponding mean squared errors were approximately
\[
\mathrm{MSE}_{\text{train}} \approx 0.068, \qquad
\mathrm{MSE}_{\text{val}} \approx 0.060.
\]

In this implementation, no explicit stability constraint on the Koopman operator was imposed, i.e., a standard linear layer was used instead of a constrained SVD parameterization.

The identified Koopman model was then evaluated in open loop on the available test trajectory. The resulting open-loop error was
\[
\mathrm{RMSE}_{\mathrm{OL}}^{\mathrm{Koop}} = 1.6681\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}.
\]

The results indicate that the Koopman model achieves a slightly lower prediction error compared to the first-order model. The difference is relatively small, which suggests that both models capture the dominant system dynamics, while the Koopman model provides a marginal improvement due to its ability to represent nonlinear effects.

The identified matrices $A_{\mathcal K}$, $B_{\mathcal K}$, and $C_{\mathcal K}$ were extracted directly from the trained model parameters as
\[
A_{\mathcal K} = K.\mathrm{weight}, \qquad
B_{\mathcal K} = f_u.\mathrm{weight}, \qquad
C_{\mathcal K} = f_y^{-1}.\mathrm{weight},
\]
with $D_{\mathcal K}=0$, and are used in the following sections for Koopman-based MPC design and experimental evaluation on the PCT23 system.

For a direct visual comparison, Figure~\ref{fig:open_loop_compare} shows the measured test output together with the open-loop predictions of both identified models. The linear model follows the dominant trend of the measured response well, while the Koopman model provides a comparable prediction quality. The figure confirms that both models are suitable for subsequent controller design, although both accumulate prediction error over longer horizons.

\section{MPC Controller Design}
\label{sec:mpc_design}

Based on the identified process models described in the previous sections, MPC was designed for both the classical
Strejc model and the data-driven Koopman model. In both cases, the control
objective is to regulate the outlet temperature of the heat exchanger
around a fixed operating point. The reference was set to zero in the
normalized coordinates, corresponding to the empirical mean temperature
used during model identification.


\subsection{Koopman-based MPC}

For the Koopman-based controller, the prediction model is given by the
Koopman system matrices identified in
equations~\eqref{eq:koopman_state}, \eqref{eq:koopman_output}.
The lifted state vector has dimension $n_z = 10$, corresponding to the
learned latent representation of the measured temperature and input.


Since only the outlet temperature $T_4$ is measured, a KF is used
to estimate the lifted Koopman state.
The filter was implemented
using constant covariance matrices
\[
Q_{\mathrm{KF}} = 0.5\,I, \qquad R_{\mathrm{KF}} = 0.1,
\]
which were selected empirically to balance responsiveness.


To evaluate closed-loop performance over the entire experiment of length $N_{\mathrm{CL}}$ samples, the cumulative cost was computed as
\begin{equation}
    J_{\mathrm{CL}} =
    \sum_{t=0}^{N_{\mathrm{CL}}-1}
    \left(
        \|y_t\|_{Q}^{2} + \|u_t\|_{R}^{2}
    \right),
    \label{eq:closed_loop_cost}
\end{equation}
with weights $Q = 10$ and $R = 1$.



Input and output constraints were enforced directly in the optimization
problem. The pump speed was limited to the interval
\[
20~\% \leq u_k \leq 100~\%,
\]
and the outlet temperature was constrained to
\[
0~^\circ\mathrm{C} \leq y_k \leq 70~^\circ\mathrm{C}.
\]

The resulting quadratic program was solved online using the \texttt{quadprog} solver via the YALMIP~\cite{lofberg2004yalmip} optimization framework.

\subsection{Strejc-based MPC}


The Strejc-based MPC uses the same controller structure as the
Koopman-based MPC. In particular, the cost function weights
$(Q = 10,\, R = 1)$, the actuator and output constraints, and the
KF parameters $(Q_{\mathrm{KF}}, R_{\mathrm{KF}})$ as well as the prediction horizon $N$ were chosen
identically. 

By keeping the MPC formulation and tuning parameters consistent across
both controllers, any observed differences in control performance can be
attributed primarily to the quality of the underlying prediction model
rather than to differences in controller design.

\section{Simulation Results}
\label{sec:simulation_results}

Closed-loop simulations were performed to compare the Koopman-based MPC and the MPC based on a linear model identified from the step response under the same constraints and tuning described in Section~\ref{sec:mpc_design}. The simulations were carried out over a horizon of $150$ time steps, and both controllers were tested from several initial outlet temperatures
\[
T_0 \in \{45,50,55,58,60,62,66,68\}\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}.
\]

As a simulation plant, a nonlinear data-driven baseline model was used. This model was identified in Python using the NeuroMANCER framework and represents the outlet temperature dynamics in a lifted latent space. In contrast to the simplified linear model used for comparison, the baseline model combines neural-network-based lifting and reconstruction with linear latent-state evolution, which allows it to capture nonlinear input--output behavior more accurately.

The baseline model consists of four main components:
\begin{itemize}
    \item an encoder neural network mapping the measured outlet temperature $y$ into a latent state $x \in \mathbb{R}^{24}$,
    \item a linear latent-state transition operator $K$,
    \item a linear input lifting map $B$,
    \item a decoder neural network reconstructing the physical output from the latent state.
\end{itemize}

The latent dynamics are described by the discrete-time model
\begin{equation}
x_{k+1} = Kx_k + Bu_k,
\end{equation}
where $x_k$ denotes the lifted latent state and $u_k$ is the control input. The physical output is reconstructed as
\begin{equation}
y_k = f_{\mathrm{dec}}(x_k),
\end{equation}
where $f_{\mathrm{dec}}(\cdot)$ denotes the decoder network.

For comparison, the second controller was constructed using a linear model identified from the step response. This model provides a simplified linear approximation of the process dynamics around the operating region and is used as the prediction model inside the corresponding MPC controller.

It should be emphasized that these closed-loop simulations are used primarily to verify that both predictive controllers are able to steer the simulated plant to the desired steady-state operating region in a stable and consistent manner. Therefore, the simulation study serves mainly as a comparative validation of controller behavior on a common baseline model and is not intended to exactly reproduce the behavior of the real experimental device.

A representative example is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:cl_response45} for the initial temperature $T_0 = 45\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$, i.e., for an initial condition significantly below the steady-state operating region. In this case both controllers first apply a strong control action close to the actuator limit and then gradually reduce the input as the output approaches steady state. The responses confirm that both MPC formulations are able to steer the system from a large initial deviation toward the desired operating region. This case is used mainly to illustrate the stabilizing behavior of the controllers and to confirm that the designed MPC schemes regulate the simulated plant to steady state even for more demanding initial conditions.

\begin{figure}[!t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{images/IC_01_y0_45_00.png}
    \caption{Closed-loop response and control input comparison for Koopman MPC and MPC based on the linear model identified from step response for the initial condition $T_0 = 45\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$. This case illustrates that both controllers regulate the simulated plant to the steady-state operating region also from a large initial deviation.}
    \label{fig:cl_response45}
\end{figure}

To evaluate the closed-loop performance, three metrics were considered: the root mean square error (RMSE), the integral of absolute error (IAE), and the closed-loop quadratic objective value. The IAE is defined as
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{IAE} = \sum_{k=1}^{N} \left| y(k) - y_{\mathrm{ss}} \right|,
\label{eq:iae}
\end{equation}
where $y(k)$ denotes the outlet temperature at time step $k$, $y_{\mathrm{ss}}$ is the steady-state operating point, and $N$ is the simulation horizon.

For the initial condition $T_0 = 45\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$, the Koopman MPC achieved $\mathrm{RMSE}=4.58\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$, $\mathrm{IAE}=358.28$, and $J_{\mathrm{CL}}=563.53$, whereas the MPC based on the linear model identified from the step response achieved $\mathrm{RMSE}=4.62\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$, $\mathrm{IAE}=380.57$, and $J_{\mathrm{CL}}=562.36$. Although the quadratic objective values are almost identical in this case, the Koopman MPC yields slightly smaller tracking errors in both RMSE and IAE.

Table~\ref{tab:performance_metrics} summarizes the results for all tested initial temperatures. For initial conditions close to the operating point, both controllers achieve very small tracking errors. As the initial temperature moves further away from the reference, both RMSE and IAE increase, which is expected due to larger transient deviations and stronger control action requirements.

\section{Experimental Results}
\label{sec:experimental_results}

Experimental validation was performed on the Armfield PCT23 heat exchanger described in Section~\ref{sec:practical_introduction}.
The goal was to verify the closed-loop behavior observed in simulations on the real device and to compare the Koopman-based MPC with the MPC based on a first-order linear model under identical experimental conditions.

The experiments were initialized at different outlet temperatures.
Before each MPC run, the desired starting temperature was achieved using a PI controller acting on the pump speed.

Regulation was performed to zero in normalized coordinates.
The corresponding reference temperature $x_{\text{target}} = 63.808,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ was computed from the measured steady-state mean obtained during the experimental day.
This value is consistent with the input mean $u_{\text{mean}}$ used during identification, but it does not correspond to the mean of the identification dataset itself.
The prediction horizon, cost function weights, constraints, and Kalman filter parameters were kept identical for both controllers, so that only the difference between the prediction models is compared.

During the experiments, the outlet temperature $T_4$ was used as the feedback signal.
The heater loop and the pump in the secondary circuit were kept unchanged.
The only manipulated variable was the pump speed controlled by the MPC.

\begin{figure}[!ht]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{images/compare_cl_T0_55.png}
\caption{Experimental closed-loop response and control input for the initial temperature $T_0 = 55,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$.}
\label{fig:cl_exp_T0_55}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:cl_exp_T0_55} shows the experimental closed-loop response for the case starting from
$T_0 = 55,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$.
Both controllers regulate the outlet temperature towards the operating point and reach a stable steady-state region.

At the beginning of the experiment, both controllers apply a strong control action close to the actuator limits.
The Koopman MPC remains at the upper input constraint slightly longer and exhibits a more aggressive behavior, which leads to a small overshoot in the temperature.
The linear MPC reduces the input earlier and follows a smoother trajectory towards the steady state.

In terms of performance metrics, both controllers achieve comparable results.
For $T_0 = 55,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$, the RMSE is $2.004,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for the Koopman MPC and $1.872,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ for the linear MPC.
The IAE values are $272.46$ and $296.91$, respectively.
The objective function value is lower for the linear MPC, which corresponds to smoother control actions and smaller input variations.

The differences can be related to the behavior of the real system.
The heat exchanger exhibits nonlinearities and disturbances that are not fully captured by either model.
The Koopman MPC tends to react more strongly when the temperature is below the expected value, which results in larger initial control actions and occasional overshoot.
The linear MPC behaves more conservatively, which can lead to slightly lower RMSE but slower adjustment of the input.

Overall, both controllers provide consistent and stable regulation of the system.
The main differences are visible in the transient phase, particularly in the aggressiveness of the control input and the speed of convergence.

\subsection{Transient Performance Evaluation}
\label{sec:transient_evaluation}

Since many of the measured closed-loop responses enter a steady-state
region before the end of the full $300$-sample experiment, we also focused separately on the evaluation
of the transient phase. It's the time it takes for the output to get close to the steady-state. 

The end of the transient was determined as the first time instant when
the output entered a small band around the target value and stayed inside
this band for at least $N_{\mathrm{hold}} = 5$ consecutive samples. 
The width of this band was selected based on the noise level observed in
the measured data. From steady-state parts of the identification
experiments, the temperature signal varied approximately within
$\pm 0.2\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$. 
Based on this observation, the band was set to twice this variation,
resulting in a band of $\pm 0.4\,^\circ\mathrm{C}$ around the target value.

For each experiment, the transient interval was defined from the initial
sample until this time. The following metrics were evaluated over this
interval:

\begin{itemize}
    \item settling time, defined as the first time instant at which the
    output enters the settling band around the target value and remains
    within this band for at least $N_{\mathrm{hold}} = 5$ consecutive samples,

    \item integral of absolute error (IAE), defined according to
    \eqref{eq:iae},

    \item integral of squared error (ISE), defined as
    \begin{equation}
    \mathrm{ISE} = \sum_{k=1}^{N} \left( y(k) - y_{\mathrm{ss}} \right)^2,
    \end{equation}

    \item integral of time-weighted absolute error (ITAE), defined as
    \begin{equation}
    \mathrm{ITAE} = \sum_{k=1}^{N} k \cdot \left| y(k) - y_{\mathrm{ss}} \right|,
    \end{equation}

    \item transient objective value $J_{\mathrm{CL}}$, evaluated
    according to \eqref{eq:closed_loop_cost}, where $N_{\mathrm{CL}}$
    corresponds to the length of the transient phase, i.e., the time until
    the settling condition is satisfied.
\end{itemize}

settling instant for both controllers by vertical lines, which define the

    \section*{Conclusions}
    \label{sec:conclusions}

    This work dealt with modeling and control of a plate heat exchanger using two different prediction models within an MPC framework. A classical first-order Strejc model identified from step responses was compared with a data-driven Koopman model obtained from measured data using a neural-network-based lifting. Both models were used as prediction models in MPC controllers with identical structure, constraints, and tuning, which allows a direct comparison of their behavior.
    
    System identification is described in Section~\ref{sec:strejc_ident} for the Strejc model and in Section~\ref{sec:koopman_identification} for the Koopman model. The Strejc model captures the dominant dynamics of the heat exchanger with a simple structure and provides stable predictions around the operating point. The Koopman model represents the system in a higher-dimensional lifted space and is able to describe the nonlinear behavior of the heat exchanger directly from data.
    
    The MPC design is presented in Section~\ref{sec:mpc_design}. Both controllers were formulated in normalized coordinates with regulation to zero, corresponding to a chosen operating temperature. The same prediction horizon, cost function weights, constraints, and Kalman filter parameters were used for both controllers so that differences in closed-loop behavior are caused by the prediction models and not by controller tuning.
    
    Closed-loop simulations in Section~\ref{sec:simulation_results} show that both controllers regulate the outlet temperature of the heat exchanger without instability. For initial conditions close to the operating point, the tracking error is small for both approaches. For larger deviations, the Koopman MPC typically achieves lower RMSE and IAE values. This is mainly related to a more aggressive initial control action, which drives the system faster towards the reference. The Strejc MPC applies smoother input changes, which often leads to a slightly lower or comparable objective function value, even when the tracking error is higher.
    
    Experimental results in Section~\ref{sec:experimental_results} confirm these observations on the real heat exchanger. Both controllers work well on the physical system and are able to regulate the outlet temperature under real disturbances and measurement noise. The performance metrics are mostly consistent with the simulation results. Compared to simulation, the control signals on the real heat exchanger are more irregular, mainly due to disturbances and dynamics that are not captured by the models.
    This is particularly visible for the Koopman MPC, where the first control move can be estimated more aggressively.



    The comparison shows that the Koopman-based MPC usually achieves lower RMSE, especially at the beginning of regulation. This behavior is mainly caused by a more aggressive initial control action. The Strejc-based MPC is based on a simpler model and produces smoother input trajectories, which can be an advantage when large or fast changes of the manipulated variable are unwanted.

    In this work, the Koopman model was identified for a SISO configuration, with Pump~F as the manipulated variable and the outlet temperature $T_4$ as the controlled output. A change of the controlled variable, manipulated input, or operating regime would require a new identification step. An advantage of the Koopman framework is that the model can be updated directly from measured data, while the MPC formulation can remain unchanged.
    
    Overall, both MPC approaches are applicable to the plate heat exchanger. The results indicate a trade-off between model simplicity and achievable tracking performance, with the Koopman-based MPC providing lower tracking errors and the Strejc-based MPC producing smoother control actions.
